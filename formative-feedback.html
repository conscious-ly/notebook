Formative Feedback

Testing

For our Formative Feedback, we were assigned the evaluation of Embedded Art.

As recommended in the formative feedback, user testing was performed on large groups in a “naturalistic environment”, varied under three different settings:

  1. In a semi-social group studying environment
  2. In a social “rest and relaxation” lounge
  3. At a gathering in a restaurant

This exposed the prototype to a total of 66 users. At least two testers were present at all times to oversee the prototype; one tester kept watch of the prototype while the other recorded results. Moreover, quantitative and qualitative evaluation criteria from the high-fidelity prototype section were converted into a Google form to make it easier to collect data. Note that in the form, “Person 1” refers to the same user, and details of users who did not interact with the system at all were omitted (but their presence was noted). The results of the test in each setting can be viewed individually in the link below. Lastly, a video recording of the test sessions was also cross-referenced where it was possible to record.

Qualitative Results

Overall, it seems that using the prototype was an enjoyable experience for the users, as they used it as both a utility (a charging station) and it sparked interesting conversations related to the art piece.

At times, users expressed frustration by the lack of charging ports. The main issue faced when testing was the users’ inability to recognize what function the prototype was meant to serve. One user thought it was “a shrine for your laptop”. Another user (shown in the video below at 0:55) completely ignored the prototype and sat down in front of it.

If the video does not play (which will most likely be the case due to it being hosted on McGill's server), you can still view the video here.

A remark that was made multiple times was “I feel like my phone might get hacked”. This is quite understandable, and likely due to the recent buzz in the news about “juice jacking”, where charging stations are set up by hackers to steal data from users’ devices. Security concerns aside, one of the main similarities amongst the test subjects was their appreciation for the charging feature that the prototype provided. Conversely, the key difference observed between the three tests was that users in the group-study setting did not entertain the art or conversational aspect of the prototype at all, only seeming to see it as a charging station, while users in the other groups did.

Failed Tests

Though this test wasn’t one of the original ones outlined by the design team, the following test under the Usability Goal 2 (The users should quickly understand that the system requires their phones) implied that at least one user would plug their phone in without prompt:

Based on our observations during the test and our interaction with the users afterwards, some of the reasons users didn’t plug their phones in without prompting were:

  • Security - mentioned above
  • The look of the frame - some users felt that were the frame made out of a sleeker and less “unfinished” material such as cardboard, they would have been more inclined to approach it. This was predicted by the design team, and they had already suggested that they may even take a frameless approach in later versions
  • The positioning of the device - in the second test, the users mentioned that because the device was placed near another student, the users thought it belonged to someone and wasn’t meant to be approached
  • The image itself - some users didn’t understand what the prototype was; and thought the screen was broken

Note that there were other usability issues we came across, which we have detailed and ranked by priority in the Design Critique section.

Heuristic Evaluation

The following is the list of Heuristics we evaluated based on the high-fidelity prototype heuristic evaluation criteria, omitting those that were not considered a priority. Overall, we found that the heuristics chosen were appropriate for evaluating this prototype. We categorized each heuristic into either a violation or a strength and gave rating based Jakob Nielson four-step scale:

  1. This is not a usability problem at all
  2. Cosmetic problem only: need not be fixed unless extra time is available on project
  3. Minor usability problem: fixing this should be given low priority
  4. Major usability problem: important to fix, so should be given high priority
  5. Usability catastrophe: imperative to fix this before product can be released

Heuristic

Severity & Rating

Description

Visibility of system status

Violation - 5

There is no signifier as to what the status of the system is when there are no phones plugged in: the screen simply remains blank and does not provide users with any visibility to it’s status. When the image is starting to become more visible, users are not able to intuitively form a conceptual model that maps each plugged in phone to a slightly clearer image

Match between system and the real world

Strength - 2

While the system fit in a casual environment for the most part, as stated earlier some users were put off by the appearance of the prototype

User control and freedom

Strength - 2

While users were free to plug-in and remove their phones as they please, this could be hampered by the limited number of ports and the physical placement of the prototype

Error prevention

Strength - 1

The prototype was designed in a way that it’s almost impossible for a user to misuse it or perform an error

Recognition rather than recall

Violation - 3

Despite the ubiquitous use of USB charging stations across campus and users’ ability to recognize that they need to plug-in their phones, users’ were still unable to make sense of the purpose of the screen, which was compounded by the lack of any signifiers

Aesthetic and minimalist design

Strength - 1

The prototype possessed the minimum amount of design elements required to carry out it’s functionality

Help and documentation

Violation - 5

Of the total 66 users tested, not a single user approached the system without some kind of prompt or explanations from the testing team. This implies that there’s a strong need for a mechanism to aid users in understanding the intended purpose of the system

Test Plan Critique

Test Cases vs Usability Goals

The design team suggested 6 quantitative tests which corresponded to 5 usability goals. Our evaluation of their relationship is as follows:

Quantitative Test

Usability Goal

Evaluation of test with regards to the associated UG

Number of users that go to the frame

[UG 1] Users should quickly notice the presence of the system

This test is appropriate for its respective usability goal.

Average time it takes for a user to go to picture frame

[UG 1] Users should quickly notice the presence of the system

This test is appropriate for its respective usability goal.

Time spent for users to start connecting their phones

[UG 2] The users should quickly understand that the system requires their phones

We (the evaluation team) feel that this test is incomplete, as it assumes that users would connect their phones. As a precursor to this test, another test should have been associated to this usability goal: the number of people who did connect their phone to the prototype. When we did our testing, we made sure to add that in.

Number of users that speak to each other

[UG 3] The system should disengage users from their phones

This test is appropriate for its respective usability goal

If they speak to each other after finishing with the frame

[UG 4] The system should encourage conversation

This test is appropriate for its respective usability goal

Time spent at picture frame

[UG 5] The system should be fun and/or entertaining

We feel that this test is not fully representative of the usability goal, as not all time spent at the frame is a result of it being entertaining/fun (for example, even in a social/party setting, users may stay near the frame simply to watch over their phone as it charged). A better-suited quantitative test would be: the amount of time spent at the frame discussing it with others, and/or the number of users who verbally showed interest in the image itself.

One addition we suggest for the design team concerning the quantitative tests: provide success/fail metrics and define expectations with regards to each of the tests.

Additions to the Test Procedure

When carrying out the tests, we followed the guidelines provided by the design team, but also made changes and additions:

  • We separated our tests based on differences in setting, as opposed to testing multiple users. It was unclear whether or not we were supposed to test against user population or if we were supposed to record results for each individual user. We felt that setting was the most appropriate factor to differentiate each round of tests, loosely based on user population.
  • We distinguished between users who approached the frame but did not plug their phone in, and those who did, and recorded the number of people for both.
  • We distinguished between users who plugged their phone in without prompting and with prompting. Note that this was in response to the assumption made by the test associated to the second usability goal.
